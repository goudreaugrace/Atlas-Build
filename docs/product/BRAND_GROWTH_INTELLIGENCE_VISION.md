# PepsiCo Brand Growth Intelligence Vision

## Status

Working proposal. This document captures the larger vision that emerged from the BBE Brand Doctor prototype, the Day 3 workshop transcript, and the Ehrenberg-Bass Mental Availability Tracking report shared with the team.

This is not a replacement for BBE. Better Brand Equity remains the diagnostic spine. This is a proposal for an intelligence layer that connects BBE to supporting systems such as How Brands Grow, Mental Availability, Growth Navigator, physical availability, AI-era discoverability, and human brand judgment so teams can move from equity diagnosis to better marketing action.

## Executive Thesis

Brand Doctor should become the first doorway into a broader PepsiCo Brand Growth Intelligence Layer, while staying grounded in Better Brand Equity.

The larger product is not a dashboard, a report generator, a generic chatbot, or a wrapper around BBE. It is an operating layer that helps PepsiCo teams answer:

- Is this brand easy to think of?
- Is it easy to recognize?
- Is it easy to find and buy?
- Is it gaining or losing momentum?
- Which category entry points should it build, defend, or avoid?
- What evidence supports that read?
- What should the team do next?
- How will we know whether momentum is building?

The sharpened product thesis:

> PepsiCo Brand Growth Intelligence turns BBE-led diagnosis and connected brand data into science-grounded marketing judgment.

The system should help insights planners, brand managers, and senior leaders move from data production to growth provocation. It should make the science usable, make the evidence visible, and make the next decision clearer.

## Why The Vision Expanded

The original prototype scope was intentionally single-brand and diagnosis-first:

```text
Find My Brand
-> Brand Health Panel
-> Diagnosis
-> Evidence
-> Dialog With Data
-> Treatment Plan
-> Follow-Up Signals
```

The room moved beyond that scope because the prototype made three things tangible:

1. The team could see a system that was not just summarizing data, but starting to reason across data, logic, evidence, and action.
2. Voice and chat made the product feel like a future work mode, not a static report.
3. The "no magic" principle made AI feel usable in a skeptical expert environment.

The important evolution is that Brand Doctor is no longer the full ambition, but BBE remains the anchor. The larger intelligence system should make BBE more useful, more trusted, and more actionable rather than asking marketers to adopt a competing framework.

### Ehrenberg-Bass Mental Availability Tracking Report

The report adds a more rigorous operating model for the HBG foundation. It makes Mental Availability measurable through:

- Mental Market Share: total share of mental structures across category entry points and buyers.
- Mental Penetration: percent of category buyers linking the brand to at least one category entry point.
- Network Size: average number of category entry points linked to the brand among those with mental penetration.
- Share of Mind: competitive strength among those who know the brand.
- Category Entry Point advantages and disadvantages after controlling for brand size and category relevance.
- Category Entry Point relevance and mental competition.
- Buyer versus non-buyer splits.

This is the clearest bridge from How Brands Grow philosophy to practical product architecture.

## Product Reframe

### From

BBE reports and deep dives are difficult to interpret and action consistently.

### To

BBE Brand Doctor helps marketers understand the BBE diagnosis, believe the evidence, connect the read to supporting systems, and choose better treatment paths to test.

### Brand Doctor Role

Brand Doctor remains the brand-level diagnostic experience.

It answers:

- What condition is the brand in?
- Why do we believe it?
- What evidence is missing?
- What treatment paths should be considered?
- What follow-up signals should prove or challenge the read?

### Intelligence Layer Role

The intelligence layer sits around Brand Doctor as a connected evidence and action layer.

It answers:

- How does the BBE diagnosis translate into action?
- Which supporting lens helps explain the BBE pattern: mental, distinctive, physical, machine, commercial, or portfolio?
- Which category entry points create headroom?
- Which brands should build, defend, or avoid the same mental territory?
- Which actions create momentum, not just activity?

## Proposed Operating Model

The system can organize connected evidence around six growth availability dimensions, but those dimensions should be read as support for BBE-led diagnosis and action.

### 1. Mental Availability

Question:

Is the brand easy to think of in buying situations?

Core evidence:

- Salient.
- Demand Power.
- Occasion scores.
- Category Entry Points.
- Mental Market Share.
- Mental Penetration.
- Network Size.
- Share of Mind.
- Buyer/non-buyer mental availability gaps.

Product implication:

Move from generic "awareness" language to specific mental availability reads:

- How many category buyers link the brand to any useful entry point?
- How broad is the brand's CEP network?
- Is the brand too mentally niched?
- Are non-buyers failing to link the brand to the category?
- Which CEPs are worth building versus avoiding?

### 2. Distinctive Memory Structures

Question:

Is the brand easy to recognize as itself?

Core evidence:

- Distinctive asset recognition.
- Branding quality.
- Creative asset consistency.
- Packaging recognition.
- BBE Different, used carefully.
- Creative and design system inputs when available.

Product implication:

Separate "Different" from "Distinctive."

Different can be a useful BBE metric, but the system should not push teams into narrow differentiation theater. It should ask whether the brand has recognizable assets that help people notice, encode, retrieve, and buy the brand.

### 3. Physical Availability

Question:

Is the brand easy to find and buy?

Core evidence:

- Growth Navigator bridge.
- Distribution.
- Retail presence.
- Shelf prominence.
- E-commerce search/rank.
- Pack and format availability.
- Price-pack architecture.
- Retail media conversion signals.

Product implication:

Physical Availability is not a footnote. It should be an equal diagnostic pillar. A brand can be mentally available but physically constrained, or physically present but weakly encoded in memory.

### 4. Momentum

Question:

Is the brand getting healthier, or merely large today?

Core evidence:

- BBE Momentum.
- Mental Availability change over time.
- CEP gains/losses.
- Buyer/non-buyer movement.
- GN trend signals.
- Treatment follow-up waves.

Product implication:

Momentum should be treated as a system state, not a chart. The product should help teams see whether evidence is compounding in the right direction.

Momentum states could include:

- Building momentum.
- Strong but leaking.
- Latent headroom.
- Stalled foundation.
- Availability constrained.
- Evidence missing.

### 5. AI / Machine Availability

Question:

Is the brand easy for AI systems, search engines, retailer algorithms, and answer engines to understand, retrieve, and recommend?

Core evidence:

- Structured product data.
- Retail API and product feed quality.
- Search visibility.
- AI answer visibility.
- Reviews and first-party content.
- Source authority.
- Knowledge graph/entity clarity.
- Availability of price, pack, claims, and retailer paths.

Product implication:

This is not a replacement for HBG. It is a 2026 extension of availability. Humans need to think of and buy the brand; machines increasingly mediate what gets found, summarized, recommended, and purchased.

Suggested label:

Machine Availability or AI Availability.

The system should be clear this is an applied modern layer, not an official HBG law.

### 6. Portfolio Growth Availability

Question:

Does the portfolio catch category buyers as their lives, occasions, needs, and affinities change?

Core evidence:

- Brand roles.
- Portfolio coverage by CEP.
- Overlap and whitespace.
- Category lens.
- Market and occasion shifts.
- Mental competition by CEP.
- Physical availability by channel.
- Consumer life-stage and need-state evolution.

Product implication:

The portfolio layer should not start as cannibalization proof. It should start as portfolio availability intelligence:

- Which brands cover which buying situations?
- Which CEPs are over-contested?
- Which useful CEPs are unclaimed?
- Which brands are too mentally narrow?
- Where does the portfolio fail to catch buyers as needs change?

The user asks:

The assistant answers with:

- Evidence basis.

Live Consult becomes a premium interface for the same intelligence layer:

- "Give me the boardroom read."
- "Challenge your own diagnosis."

The voice layer should never be a separate source of truth. It should call the same data, rules, citations, and guardrails.

## What "No Magic" Means In The Larger System

No Magic becomes a product principle, not a feature.

Every output should identify:

- What the data says.
- What rule or framework was applied.
- What the AI summarized, translated, or inferred.
- What evidence is missing.
- What should not be concluded.
- What would change the diagnosis.
- Which human role owns the final judgment.

Suggested evidence labels:

- Measured fact.
- Deterministic rule.
- Configured treatment logic.
- Expert-authored framework.
- AI interpretation.
- Missing evidence.
- Open assumption.

## Data Model Implications

The Brand Health Record should remain the central contract for V1, but the future data model needs new modules.

### Mental Availability Packet

```ts
type MentalAvailabilityPacket = {
  brandId: string;
  category: string;
  market: string;
  period: string;
  mentalMarketShare?: number;
  mentalPenetration?: number;
  networkSize?: number;
  shareOfMind?: number;
  salesPenetration?: number;
  buyerSplit?: {
    buyers: MentalAvailabilityMetrics;
    nonBuyers: MentalAvailabilityMetrics;
  };
  cepAdvantages: CepAssociation[];
  cepDisadvantages: CepAssociation[];
  sourceFiles: string[];
};
```

### Category Entry Point Packet

```ts
type CategoryEntryPoint = {
  id: string;
  label: string;
  category: string;
  relevance: 'high' | 'medium' | 'low';
  competition: 'high' | 'medium' | 'low' | 'free';
  linkedBrands: string[];
  portfolioImplication: string;
  source: string;
};
```

### Availability Packet

```ts
type AvailabilityPacket = {
  mentalAvailability?: MentalAvailabilityPacket;
  physicalAvailability?: PhysicalAvailabilityPacket;
  machineAvailability?: MachineAvailabilityPacket;
  distinctiveAssets?: DistinctiveAssetPacket;
};
```

The first implementation can be JSON-first and partial. Missing data should be surfaced as Evidence Readiness, not hidden.

## Methodology Guardrails

The system should be HBG-grounded but not claim official endorsement unless explicitly approved.

Guardrails:

- Do not equate BBE Different with distinctive assets.
- Do not infer physical availability without commercial evidence.
- Do not infer cannibalization or portfolio migration from CEP overlap alone.
- Do not recommend SKU-level pricing from Perceived Value.
- Do not treat one wave as causal proof.
- Do not let AI invent CEP ownership, mental advantages, or treatments.
- Do not over-target heavy buyers when the evidence suggests a penetration or light-buyer problem.
- Do not recommend broad reach without checking branding quality and distinctive assets.

## Product Principles

1. Better Brand Equity is the diagnostic spine for V1.
2. HBG is an important marketing-science philosophy that helps explain and action the BBE read.
3. Connected systems should answer: "How does this help us understand or act on the BBE diagnosis?"
4. Mental and physical availability are both required for growth, but neither should be inferred without evidence.
5. Distinctiveness is different from being different.
6. Momentum matters more than static status.
7. AI explains, interrogates, and synthesizes; deterministic/configured systems diagnose and rank.
8. Every recommendation needs evidence, caveats, and follow-up signals.
9. The system should make insights planners more influential, not obsolete.
10. The interface should feel premium, human, and future-facing.
11. The portfolio should help PepsiCo catch buyers as needs and lives evolve.

## Deployment Strategy

The safest path is not open democratization on day one.

Recommended rollout:

1. Insights planner co-pilot.
2. Brand team pilot with guided mode.
3. Leadership readout mode.
4. Portfolio intelligence beta.
5. Broader self-serve access after governance and data readiness improve.

Reason:

The tool can accelerate insight generation, but the organization still needs human judgment, influence, and change leadership. The insights planner role should shift from producing decks to owning the growth thread and selling the provocation.

Build a Growth Availability Read inside the current brand page.

It should appear near the executive summary and show:

- Mental Availability: current read and evidence basis.
- Distinctive Memory Structures: read and missing evidence.
- Physical Availability: read and missing evidence.
- Machine Availability: read and missing evidence.
- Momentum: whether the brand appears to be gaining, holding, declining, or unknown.

Example:

```text
Growth Availability Read

Mental Availability: Directional weakness
Evidence: Salient not ahead; no Mental Market Share packet loaded.

Distinctive Memory Structures: Missing evidence

Evidence: Growth Navigator bridge not available for this brand.

Machine Availability: Not yet assessed
Evidence: No AI/search/retail structured data packet loaded.

Growth Constraint: Underbuilt memory and availability evidence.
What this means: Do not jump straight to media scale. First validate the memory structures, availability gaps, and category entry points worth building.
```

This would make the vision visible without requiring the full data spine yet.
