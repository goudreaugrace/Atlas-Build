Yes — there are several elements from the EBI MMS report that would materially strengthen a transformed BBE Momentum Intelligence Report, especially if the ambition is to move BBE from a descriptive brand-equity readout into a more growth-diagnostic system.
I would not treat the EBI MMS report as a replacement for BBE. I’d use it as a Mental Availability and CEP momentum layer that helps explain why BBE measures are moving — or not moving — and whether our activity is building the right memory structures among the right buyers.
The strongest elements to incorporate
1. Add a Mental Availability KPI layer alongside BBE MDS
The EBI report breaks Mental Availability into four useful metrics:
Mental Market Share — total share of mental structures across CEPs and category buyers.
Mental Penetration — % of category buyers who link the brand to at least one CEP.
Network Size — average number of CEPs linked to the brand among those with mental penetration.
Share of Mind — the brand’s share of mental structures among those who have the brand mentally available. [Ehrenberg-...epsiCo PDF | PDF]
This would give BBE a much sharper momentum read because BBE often tells us whether a brand is Meaningful, Different or Salient, but not always whether the brand is becoming easier to retrieve across more buying situations. The MMS framework gives us that bridge. [Ehrenberg-...epsiCo PDF | PDF]
Recommended addition to BBE report:
A “Mental Availability Momentum” page showing:
MMS vs last wave / benchmark
Mental Penetration
Network Size
Share of Mind
Interpretation: is growth coming from broader buyer reach, more CEP breadth, or stronger competitive memory share?
2. Separate reach problems from messaging problems
One of the most useful parts of the EBI methodology is the diagnostic distinction between:
Lower Mental Penetration / Higher Network Size = likely media reach issue; activity may be over-concentrated among heavy or existing buyers.
Higher Mental Penetration / Lower Network Size = likely messaging issue; the brand may be widely known but linked to too narrow a range of CEPs.
If neither explains the deviation, EBI suggests reviewing branding execution quality — how easily viewers notice and recognise the brand in creative. [Ehrenberg-...epsiCo PDF | PDF]
This is highly relevant for a transformed BBE report because it creates a clearer “so what” from the data. Instead of just saying “Salience is down” or “Meaningful is weak,” we can diagnose whether the issue is reach, CEP breadth, or distinctive branding quality.
Recommended addition to BBE report:
A diagnostic box on each brand:
Is the brand’s issue primarily reach, retrieval breadth, or recognition/branding quality?
3. Use CEP mental advantages/disadvantages, not just raw attribute scores
The EBI report assesses whether a brand scores higher or lower than expected on a CEP after controlling for brand size and category membership. A difference of +5pp or more is treated as a mental advantage, while -5pp or lower is treated as a mental disadvantage. [Ehrenberg-...epsiCo PDF | PDF]
This is very useful for BBE because raw associations can be misleading: big brands naturally get more responses, and some CEPs are naturally more common. The EBI approach helps identify whether a brand is genuinely over- or under-performing relative to what should be expected. [Ehrenberg-...epsiCo PDF | PDF]
Recommended addition to BBE report:
Replace or supplement raw CEP association charts with:
Expected vs observed CEP strength
Mental Advantage / Mental Disadvantage flags
Prioritised interpretation by brand
This would make the BBE report more objective and less vulnerable to over-interpreting large-brand effects.
4. Add CEP relevance and competitiveness to prioritisation
The EBI report makes an important distinction between:
Category relevance — how often a CEP matters to category buyers, based on share of linkages.
Mental competitiveness — how many brands already have a mental advantage on that CEP. [Ehrenberg-...epsiCo PDF | PDF]
For example, the report notes that higher-competition CEPs are harder to build because multiple brands already have a mental advantage, while free or low-competition CEPs may represent messaging or portfolio opportunities if they are sufficiently relevant. [Ehrenberg-...epsiCo PDF | PDF]
This is exactly the kind of prioritisation logic we need in a transformed BBE report. It avoids the trap of choosing CEPs simply because the brand is weak on them or because they look strategically attractive.
Recommended addition to BBE report:
A CEP prioritisation matrix:
High relevance / low competition = build opportunity
High relevance / high competition = defend or invest only if strategically critical
Low relevance / low competition = selective/niche opportunity
Low relevance / high competition = deprioritise unless portfolio role demands it
This would fit very well with your Growth Navigator / BBE thinking because it makes CEP prioritisation more disciplined.
5. Include buyer vs non-buyer Mental Availability splits
The EBI report shows Mental Availability metrics separately for buyers and non-buyers, which is especially useful for growth diagnosis. For example, it states that Pepsi Max has room to improve mental availability among non-buyers, with three in five non-buyers failing to link the brand to any CEP; Pepsi has a similar issue, with two in three non-buyers not linking the brand to any CEP. [Ehrenberg-...epsiCo PDF | PDF]
This is a strong addition to BBE because penetration growth depends heavily on light and non-buyers. A brand can look strong among current buyers but still have a weak growth runway if non-buyers do not connect it to enough buying situations.
Recommended addition to BBE report:
A “Growth Audience Diagnostic”:
Buyers vs non-buyers
Mental Penetration gap
Network Size gap
Share of Mind gap
Implication for media reach and messaging
This would also help avoid over-optimising against current users.
6. Build a clearer link between BBE and the growth levers: media, branding, messaging
The EBI report frames Mental Availability building around three intervention types:
Media tactics — reach all category buyers using creativity to cut through.
Branding tactics — be easily recognisable as the brand in any environment.
Messaging tactics — build memory structures that act as useful retrieval paths. [Ehrenberg-...epsiCo PDF | PDF]
This could be very valuable in the BBE report because it turns diagnosis into action. If Salience or CEP linkage is weak, the answer is not always “change positioning.” It may be that the brand needs broader reach, stronger branding assets, or more consistent CEP-based messaging.
Recommended addition to BBE report:
For every diagnosed issue, add a recommended lever:
Reach problem → media plan / reach against category buyers
Recognition problem → distinctive brand assets / branding quality
Retrieval problem → CEP messaging / consistency over time
Availability problem → physical availability / channel presence

Bottom line
The most valuable elements to bring into the transformed BBE Momentum Intelligence Report are:
Mental Market Share as a companion to BBE Salience
Mental Penetration and Network Size to explain whether we have a reach or breadth issue
Expected vs observed CEP performance to avoid misleading raw association reads
CEP relevance and mental competitiveness to prioritise where to build
Buyer vs non-buyer splits to keep the report focused on penetration growth
A clear translation from diagnosis to media, branding and messaging actions
In short: BBE tells us how the brand is positioned in equity terms; EBI MMS helps explain whether the brand is becoming more mentally available in the buying situations that matter. That is exactly the kind of intelligence layer that would make the BBE report more momentum-oriented and more actionable.
